## I. INTRODUCTION (1 page)

### A. Hook
- Youth mental health crisis statistics
  - 1 in 3 teen girls seriously considered suicide (U.S. Surgeon General 2022)
  - 67% of teens use YouTube daily
  - #mentalhealth has 50+ billion views across platforms
- Gap: We know teens consume mental health content online, but HOW does the algorithm shape what they see?

### B. Research Context
- Social media and adolescent mental health (Twenge & Campbell, 2018)
- Algorithmic amplification (Ribeiro et al., 2020)
- Adolescent peer relations in digital context (Nesi et al., 2021)
- Nuanced effects of digital technology (Orben & Przybylski, 2019)

### C. Research Gap
- Existing research shows CORRELATION between social media and mental health outcomes
- Limited research on HOW algorithms specifically shape mental health content exposure
- No systematic study of YouTube mental health recommendation PATHWAYS
- No framework for understanding WHY some pathways are harmful and others protective

### D. Research Questions
1. How does YouTube's recommendation algorithm shape mental health content pathways for different search queries?
2. What factors determine whether algorithmic pathways lead to helpful resources or potentially harmful content?
3. Can we identify a framework for predicting pathway outcomes based on user search behavior?

### E. Contribution Statement
This research contributes:
- First systematic mapping of YouTube mental health recommendation pathways
- Novel dual-axis framework predicting algorithmic outcomes
- Taxonomy of six distinct pathway types
- Quantitative validation of qualitative findings via API data analysis
- Actionable recommendations for platform design and policy

## III. METHODOLOGY (2-3 pages)

### A. Research Design
- Mixed methods: Qualitative (algorithmic walkthrough) + Quantitative (API data analysis)
- Exploratory design: Framework emerged from data (grounded theory approach)

### B. Phase 1: Algorithmic Walkthrough (Qualitative)
- Method: Systematic navigation of YouTube recommendation pathways
- Journeys: 6 distinct search queries
- Videos analyzed: 200+ manually coded
- Protocol: Fresh incognito browser, follow top 1-2 recommendations, 2-3 minutes per video
- Documentation: Real-time notes on content type, sentiment, professional presence, patterns
- Stopping criteria: Loop formation, pattern saturation, natural endpoints

### C. Phase 2: API Data Collection (Quantitative)
- Method: YouTube Data API v3
- Sample: 525 videos across 12 search terms
- Variables collected: Video metadata, engagement metrics, channel information, content details
- Analysis: Channel ecosystem mapping, professional vs. peer classification, engagement comparison

### D. Analytical Approach
- Inductive pattern identification from journey data
- Framework development (dual-axis model)
- Quantitative validation against API dataset
- Triangulation of qualitative and quantitative findings

## IV. FINDINGS (3-4 pages)

### A. The Dual-Axis Framework
- Axis 1: Channel type (established vs. random)
- Axis 2: Search framing (negative vs. positive)
- Four quadrants with distinct outcomes
- 6/6 journeys fit predicted quadrants

### B. Loop Taxonomy
- Six pathway types identified
- Escalation, content-type loop, audience mismatch, single-source, topic drift, positive loop
- Each with distinct mechanisms and implications

### C. API Validation
- Established ecosystem quantified (8 channels, 35.4%)
- Professional content minority (24.4%) but higher engagement (4.40% vs 3.32%)
- Teen content systematically disadvantaged
- Corpus vs. curation dual problem identified

### D. Critical Findings
- Danger zone: Negative framing + random start = escalation
- Professional content algorithmically suppressed despite higher engagement
- Teen mental health content catastrophically underserved
- Algorithm optimizes for something other than engagement rate
