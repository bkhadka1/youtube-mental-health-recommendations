## KEY FINDINGS

### Finding 1: Search Language Determines Algorithmic Pathway
Different search terms for the same underlying issue (teen mental health) 
led to dramatically different recommendation patterns:
- Clinical language ("mental health") → diverse educational
- Emotional language ("feeling depressed") → entertainment/dramatic
- Help-seeking language ("therapy for teens") → parent-focused content

### Finding 2: Algorithm May Exploit Vulnerability
"Feeling depressed" - the most emotionally vulnerable search - led to 
entertainment content (films about depression) rather than help resources. 
Algorithm served content that may be engaging but not supportive.

### Finding 3: Audience Misidentification
"Therapy for teens" likely searched by adolescents seeking help, but 
algorithm assumed searcher was parent/caregiver. Content served was 
about managing teen mental health from parent perspective.

### Finding 4: Recommendation Loops
Some pathways (Journey 2) created tight loops where algorithm repeatedly 
recommended similar content, reducing content diversity and potentially 
creating echo chambers.

---

## IMPLICATIONS

**For Adolescent Help-Seeking**:
- Teens may not find appropriate resources based on how they search
- Emotional/vulnerable language may lead away from professional help
- Algorithm assumptions about searcher identity may misdirect content

**For Platform Design**:
- Need for better intent recognition (help-seeking vs. info-seeking)
- Need for "circuit breakers" to professional resources
- Need for age/role-appropriate content matching

**For Parents/Educators**:
- Search phrasing matters significantly
- Platform may not serve helpful content to struggling teens
- Need for media literacy about algorithmic recommendations

## API DATA VALIDATION

To validate findings from algorithmic walkthrough journeys, I collected 525 additional videos via YouTube Data API across the same 12 search terms.

### Established Ecosystem Confirmation

**Quantitative definition**: Channels appearing in 5+ search terms with 10+ videos total

**8 channels identified**:
1. TEDx Talks (40 videos, 11 search terms)
2. Psych2Go (34 videos, 9 terms)
3. Therapy in a Nutshell (31 videos, 8 terms)
4. Dr. Tracey Marks (27 videos, 7 terms)
5. Dr Julie (24 videos, 12 terms)
6. Mel Robbins (10 videos, 6 terms)
7. HealthyGamerGG (10 videos, 5 terms)
8. Andrew Huberman (10 videos, 7 terms)

**These 8 channels account for 35.4% of all collected videos**, confirming the existence of an algorithmic "established ecosystem."

5 of 8 are licensed mental health professionals (Therapy in a Nutshell, Dr. Tracey Marks, Dr Julie, Andrew Huberman, HealthyGamerGG).

### Professional vs. Peer Content Distribution

**Overall corpus**: 
- Professional content: 24.4% (128 videos)
- Peer/Other content: 75.6% (397 videos)

**Journey experience**:
- Journey 1: ~1% professional (1 in 90 videos)
- Journeys 2-6: Variable but always underrepresented

**Gap**: Professional content exists at 24.4% of corpus but appears at <10% in most user journeys, confirming algorithmic suppression.

### Professional Content Performance

**Engagement metrics**:
- Professional engagement rate: 4.40%
- Peer engagement rate: 3.32%
- **Professional content outperforms peer by 32.5%**

**Implication**: Algorithm is NOT simply optimizing for engagement. If it were, professional content (higher engagement) would be surfaced more frequently. Other factors (watch time, CTR, recency, diversity) likely dominate recommendation logic.

### Search Term Variation

**Professional content availability varies dramatically by search**:
- Depression help: 56% professional (highest)
- Teen mental health: 8% professional (lowest)

**Teen-specific searches**:
- Therapy for teens: 198,526 avg views (lowest)
- Teen mental health: 385,916 avg views (second lowest)
- Engagement: 1.78%-2.71% (lowest rates)

**Conclusion**: Teen mental health content systematically disadvantaged in both creation (corpus scarcity) and consumption (low views/engagement), explaining Journey 3 audience mismatch and broader platform failure for adolescent users.

### Corpus vs. Curation Problem

**Framework update**: Platform failure has TWO dimensions:

1. **Curation problem** (algorithm): Doesn't surface professional content proportional to availability
   - Example: Depression help has 56% professional content available, but Journey 2 saw very little

2. **Corpus problem** (content creation): Insufficient professional content for some searches
   - Example: Teen mental health has only 8% professional content in corpus

**Both problems require intervention**: Better algorithms AND incentivizing professional content creation for underserved topics (especially teen-specific mental health).
