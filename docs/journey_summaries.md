## KEY FINDINGS

### Finding 1: Search Language Determines Algorithmic Pathway
Different search terms for the same underlying issue (teen mental health) led to dramatically different recommendation patterns:
- Clinical language ("mental health") → diverse educational then escalation to crisis content
- Emotional language ("feeling depressed") → entertainment/dramatic
- Help-seeking language ("therapy for teens") → parent-focused content
- Wellness language ("mental wellness") → positive self-care content

### Finding 2: Algorithm Amplifies Sentiment
Search query sentiment is amplified rather than balanced:
- Negative/clinical queries → increasingly negative content
- Positive/wellness queries → maintained positive content
- Algorithm serves content that matches emotional valence, not user welfare

### Finding 3: Audience Misidentification
"Therapy for teens" likely searched by adolescents seeking help, but algorithm assumed searcher was parent/caregiver. Content served was about managing teen mental health from parent perspective.

### Finding 4: Recommendation Loops
Some pathways created tight loops where algorithm repeatedly recommended similar content, reducing content diversity and potentially creating echo chambers:
- Journey 2: Film loop by video 15-20
- Journey 4: Single-creator loop (Doc Snipes)
- Journey 5: Topic drift loop (attachment theory)

## IMPLICATIONS

**For Adolescent Help-Seeking**:
- Teens may not find appropriate resources based on how they search
- Emotional/vulnerable language may lead away from professional help
- Algorithm assumptions about searcher identity may misdirect content
- Positive framing offers protection but requires psychological literacy

**For Platform Design**:
- Need for better intent recognition (help-seeking vs. info-seeking)
- Need for "circuit breakers" to professional resources
- Need for age/role-appropriate content matching
- Crisis detection systems for escalation patterns

**For Parents/Educators**:
- Search phrasing matters significantly
- Platform may not serve helpful content to struggling teens
- Need for media literacy about algorithmic recommendations
- Teach teens to use positive/solution-focused search terms

## API DATA VALIDATION

To validate findings from algorithmic walkthrough journeys, I collected 525 additional videos via YouTube Data API across 12 search terms (collected January 25, 2026).

### Established Ecosystem Confirmation

**Quantitative definition**: Channels appearing in 5+ search terms with 10+ videos total

**8 channels identified**:
1. TEDx Talks (40 videos)
2. Psych2Go (34 videos)
3. Therapy in a Nutshell (31 videos)
4. Dr. Tracey Marks (27 videos)
5. Dr Julie (24 videos)
6. The Grateful Therapist (13 videos)
7. motivationaldoc (12 videos)
8. Mel Robbins (10 videos)
9. HealthyGamerGG (10 videos)
10. Andrew Huberman (10 videos)

**These 8 channels account for 191 videos (36.4% of the 525-video corpus)**, confirming the existence of an algorithmic "established ecosystem."

5 of 8 are licensed mental health professionals (Therapy in a Nutshell, Dr. Tracey Marks, Dr Julie, Andrew Huberman, HealthyGamerGG).

### Professional vs. Peer Content Distribution

**Overall corpus**: 
- Professional content: 24.4% (128 videos)
- Peer/Other content: 75.6% (397 videos)

**Journey experience**:
- Journey 1: ~1% professional (1 in 90 videos)
- Journey 2: ~5% professional after initial video
- Journey 3: ~97% professional (but wrong audience)
- Journey 4: 100% professional (but single source)
- Journey 5: 100% professional (but topic drift)
- Journey 6: ~20% professional

**Gap**: Professional content exists at 24.4% of corpus but appears at <10% in most user journeys (Journeys 1, 2, 6), confirming algorithmic suppression.

### Professional Content Performance

**Engagement metrics**:
- Professional engagement rate: 4.40%
- Peer engagement rate: 3.32%
- **Professional content outperforms peer by 32.5%**

**Implication**: Algorithm is NOT simply optimizing for engagement. If it were, professional content (higher engagement) would be surfaced more frequently. Other factors (watch time, CTR, recency, diversity) likely dominate recommendation logic, or algorithm actively suppresses "educational" content in favor of "entertainment."

### Search Term Variation

**Professional content availability varies dramatically by search**:
- Depression help: 56% professional (highest)
- Coping with anxiety: 42% professional
- Anxiety support: 38% professional
- Mental health: 14% professional
- Teen mental health: 8% professional (lowest)

**Engagement rate by search term**:
- How to be happy: 4.80% (highest)
- Self care mental health: 4.23%
- Mental wellness: 3.98%
- Feeling depressed: 3.76%
- Depression help: 3.52%
- Therapy for teens: 2.71%
- Teen depression: 1.93%
- Teen mental health: 1.78% (lowest - 63% gap from highest)

**Teen-specific searches systematically disadvantaged**:
- Therapy for teens: 198,526 avg views (84% below dataset mean of 1,214,920)
- Teen mental health: Lowest professional content (8%) AND lowest engagement (1.78%)
- Teen depression: Second-lowest engagement (1.93%)

**Conclusion**: Teen mental health content systematically disadvantaged in both creation (corpus scarcity: only 8-30% professional) and consumption (low views/engagement), explaining Journey 3 audience mismatch and broader platform failure for adolescent users.

### Corpus vs. Curation Dual Problem

**Framework**: Platform failure has TWO dimensions:

1. **Curation problem** (algorithmic): Doesn't surface professional content proportional to availability
   - Example: "Mental health" has 14% professional content available, but Journey 1 encountered only ~1%
   - Example: "Depression help" has 56% professional content, yet journeys rarely feature this proportion
   - Professional content suppressed despite 32.5% higher engagement

2. **Corpus problem** (content creation): Insufficient professional content for some searches
   - Example: "Teen mental health" has only 8% professional content in entire corpus
   - Example: Teen-specific content receives 84% fewer views, discouraging professional production
   - Audience mismatch (Journey 3) reflects scarcity of teen-directed professional content

**Both problems require intervention**: 
- **Curation**: Algorithmic redesign to prioritize professional resources for vulnerable searches
- **Corpus**: Incentivize professional content creation for underserved topics (especially teen-specific mental health)
